Trust, Power, and American Banking

How financial stability depended on political legitimacy over time

The Core Problem

Early national banks stabilized the U.S. economy but failed politically. This project explores why effectiveness alone was not enough.

Early National Banks

To what extent were the early national financial structures such as the First and Second Bank of the United States effective in contributing to economic stability and to what extent did their structure predict their eventual collapse?

Main Idea

Early national financial structures such as the Second Bank of the United States allowed for improved economic stability by being a financial representation for the new nation, regulating money supply, and restraining risky banking practices. However, their stabilizing influence was limited by political backlash and regional disputes, which ultimately prevented the Bank from establishing a long-term consensus.

Evidence

  • It was a fiscal agent, holding federal deposits and issuing debt, and it engaged in bank supervision by issuing and redeeming bank notes that were deposited in state-chartered banks.
  • Nicholas Biddle took the reins. His sound management is credited with contributing to economic growth.
  • Jackson successfully portrayed the Bank as an elitist and “privileged” institution that benefitted the few.

Primary Source: Alexander Hamilton's Report (1790)

Hamilton argued for a national bank to solve economic instability, providing greater facility to the government and augmenting productive capital.

Value: Provides insight into early policymaking.

Limitations: Biased towards strong federal system; idealistic without real-world evidence.

Inequality and Political Division

In what ways did early American economic practices create enduring patterns of inequality and political division and how were these tensions eventually overcome?

Main Idea

Early American economic practices generated decent living standards but simultaneously entrenched deep structural inequality, specifically slavery, regional imbalance, and uneven access to skills and property.

Evidence

  • Slavery disappeared from half the nation but remained vital to the economy.
  • Colonial America was egalitarian, with richest 1% getting 8.5% of income in 1774.
  • Long steep rise in inequality from 1800 to 1860.

Inequality Over Time

Counterargument: Andrew Jackson's Bank Veto (1832)

Jackson argued the Bank privileged elites and harmed ordinary Americans.

Value: Offers perspective on class tensions and regional disputes.

Limitations: Political bias, appeals to emotion rather than fact.

The Federal Reserve

How did the implementation of the Federal Reserve System lessen the previous tensions in having a central economic system?

Main Idea

The Federal Reserve System reduced tensions by having a decentralized but connected structure, balancing national oversight with regional representation. It demonstrated flexibility during crises like the 2008 Financial Crisis.

Evidence

  • Used open market operations, quantitative easing, and liquidity provision during 2008 crisis.
  • Consists of 12 regional banks to protect against concentrated political pressure.
  • Autonomy from direct political control while created by Congress.

The Fed's response to crises showed its adaptability, using tools like buying bonds to stimulate the economy.
